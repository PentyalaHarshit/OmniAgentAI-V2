Budgeting and Emergency Fund Guidance

50/30/20 Rule:
- 50% of income for needs (housing, food, utilities, transportation)
- 30% of income for wants (entertainment, dining out, hobbies)
- 20% of income for savings and debt repayment

Emergency Fund Recommendations:
- Minimum: 3 months of essential expenses
- Recommended: 6 months of essential expenses
- High risk situations: 9-12 months (self-employed, variable income)

Essential Expenses Include:
- Housing (rent/mortgage, utilities, insurance)
- Food (groceries, not dining out)
- Transportation (car payment, gas, insurance)
- Minimum debt payments
- Healthcare costs
- Childcare (if applicable)

Building Emergency Fund:
1. Start with $1,000 mini emergency fund
2. Build to 3 months of expenses
3. Work toward 6 months of expenses
4. Keep in high-yield savings account for accessibility

When to Use Emergency Fund:
- Medical emergencies
- Job loss
- Major car repairs
- Essential home repairs
- Unexpected tax bills

When NOT to Use Emergency Fund:
- Planned expenses (vacations, gifts)
- Non-essential purchases
- Investment opportunities
- Paying off low-interest debt

Debt Payoff Strategies:
1. Debt Avalanche: Pay highest interest rate first
   - Mathematically optimal
   - Saves most money
   - May take longer to see progress

2. Debt Snowball: Pay smallest balance first
   - Psychological wins
   - Builds momentum
   - May cost more in interest

3. Hybrid Approach: Balance between interest and balance
   - Pay minimum on all debts
   - Extra payment to highest interest above threshold
   - Switch to snowball for small balances

Budgeting Methods:
- Zero-Based Budgeting: Every dollar has a job
- Envelope Method: Cash-based spending categories
- App-Based Tracking: Automated expense tracking
- Percentage-Based: Fixed percentages for categories

Signs You Need to Adjust Budget:
- Consistently overspending in categories
- No savings each month
- Using credit cards for essentials
- Can't handle unexpected expenses
- High stress about money
